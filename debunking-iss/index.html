The ISS, Satellites, and Live Video from Space

You can see the ISS with your own eyes

The International Space Station is visible to the naked eye from any location on Earth. Websites like NASA's Spot the Station provide exact times and sky positions — and they are accurate to the second.

If the ISS were a fake projection or high-altitude plane, its trajectory would not follow the precise orbital mechanics predicted by Kepler's laws. Amateur astronomers photograph it nightly with telescopes, capturing detail of its solar panels.

Satellites and GPS

The Global Positioning System consists of 31+ operational satellites in medium Earth orbit at ~20,200 km altitude. Your phone uses triangulation from multiple satellites simultaneously to determine your position to within a few meters.

This only works if:

  • The satellites exist
  • They are where orbital mechanics predicts they will be
  • The speed-of-light delays match the distances involved

Flat earth models cannot account for GPS accuracy, nor for the existence of geostationary satellites (which must be directly above the equator and orbit at ~35,786 km to remain stationary relative to Earth's surface).

Live footage

Multiple space agencies — NASA, ESA, JAXA, Roscosmos — as well as private companies like SpaceX broadcast live, unedited footage from space. Independent amateur astronomers point telescopes at the ISS and livestream independently. The curvature of Earth is visible in all footage taken from sufficient altitude.
