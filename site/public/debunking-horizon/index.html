The Horizon: Your First Clue the Earth is Round

The vanishing point illusion vs. actual curvature

Flat earthers claim that objects disappear at the horizon due to "perspective" — that is, they simply get too small to see. This is demonstrably false and easy to test.

The experiment anyone can run

Take a camera with a zoom lens to the ocean. Watch a ship sail away. Notice that the hull disappears before the mast. This is not perspective — zooming in restores the full size of the ship's upper structure, but the bottom remains hidden.

This happens because the Earth curves downward away from you at a rate of roughly 8 inches per mile squared. At 10 miles distance, the surface has dropped approximately 66 feet below your line of sight.

The Bedford Level Experiment

In 1838, Samuel Rowbotham conducted the famous Bedford Level experiment, claiming to show the Earth was flat. In 1870, John Hampden's wager led Alfred Russel Wallace to repeat it correctly — using proper controls for atmospheric refraction — conclusively proving curvature.

Modern laser experiments and high-resolution cameras have replicated this hundreds of times. The curvature is measurable and consistent with a spherical Earth of radius ~6,371 km.

The math

For an observer at height h meters, the horizon distance d in kilometers is:

d ≈ √(2Rh)

where R = 6,371 km. For a person 1.7m tall, the horizon is ~4.7 km away. This formula has been verified by sailors, surveyors, and aircraft pilots for centuries.
